Anthropic Had Two Different Leak Stories

March's Anthropic confusion came from two separate failures: a public CMS leak exposed draft material on Claude Mythos, while a later release packaging issue exposed part of Claude Code's internal source. The Mythos leak moved markets because it previewed a stronger cyber model in testing, while the Claude Code leak showed how fast ordinary distribution mistakes can surface internal product mechanics.

2 of 10

Claude Code Leaked, Then Got Rebuilt in Public

Anthropic said a release packaging issue exposed part of Claude Code's internal source, including thousands of TypeScript files that were quickly mirrored across GitHub. Then projects like claw-code began building clean-room Python and Rust versions with local run instructions; in practice, clean-room means recreating behavior without copying original source, which helps reduce infringement risk. The episode shows that once AI tooling internals leak, communities can operationalize alternatives extremely quickly.

3 of 10

Microsoft Breaks the One-Model AI Stack

Microsoft's March Copilot Cowork launch showed the enterprise AI stack is becoming multi-model by design, with Claude powering long-running work inside Microsoft 365. The new $99 per user per month E7 tier bundles Copilot, identity controls, and Agent 365 governance, turning AI oversight into a product line rather than an afterthought. For OpenAI, the message is blunt: distribution inside Office now matters as much as having the biggest model.

4 of 10

A Physicist Used Claude to Do a Year of Research in Two Weeks

Harvard physicist Matthew Schwartz supervised Claude as an "AI grad student" on a high-energy theory project completed in about two weeks instead of roughly a year. Anthropic reports the workflow used 110 drafts, about 36 million tokens, and more than 40 hours of expert oversight. Called "vibe physics" in Anthropic's write-up, the case is a concrete sign that frontier AI can accelerate serious scientific workflows when tightly supervised.

5 of 10

Small Models Turned Into a Price War

OpenAI's GPT-5.4 mini and nano turned March into a price-performance war for production AI. GPT-5.4 mini is priced at $0.75 per 1M input tokens and $4.50 per 1M output tokens, while nano falls to $0.20/$1.25 for high-volume workloads. OpenAI is explicitly pitching these models as subagents, which means lower pricing is now shaping system architecture, not just procurement spreadsheets.

6 of 10

AI Hiring Pressure Is No Longer Theoretical

By late March, industry trackers had logged more than 59,000 tech layoffs in 2026, with a visible share of cuts linked by executives to AI-driven productivity plans. Leaders are increasingly pitching smaller AI-assisted teams as a margin story rather than a temporary reset. For engineering and product orgs, AI strategy is now tied directly to workforce planning, reskilling, and who gets hired next.

7 of 10

Codex Is Expanding Beyond Coding

GPT-5.3-Codex is no longer just a coding autocomplete; OpenAI says it can act as a computer-based collaborator across coding, research, slides, and spreadsheets. The model runs 25% faster in Codex, sets a new high on SWE-Bench Pro, and is OpenAI's first model rated High capability for cyber tasks. That combination makes it a useful tool and a reminder that coding agents are becoming general-purpose operators.

8 of 10

AI Adoption Is Real, and So Are the Fears

Anthropic's study of 80,508 users across 159 countries shows AI adoption is already deeper than most boardrooms admit. 81% said AI had already moved them toward their goals, yet the top worries were unreliability, jobs, and autonomy rather than sci-fi catastrophe. The core tension is now visible: people want AI to save time and expand opportunity, while fearing dependence and economic displacement from the same systems.

9 of 10

AI Agents Are Starting to Spend Money

Visa says hundreds of real agent-initiated purchases have already happened in live environments, which moves agentic commerce from concept to infrastructure. Users grant upstream spending permissions, while tokenization and the Trusted Agent Protocol are supposed to separate legitimate agents from bots. The unresolved issue is liability: when an AI agent buys the wrong thing, the payment stack now has a new party to blame.

10 of 10

AI Security Work Is Getting Alarmingly Real

Anthropic and Mozilla showed what defender-side AI looks like when it works. Claude Opus 4.6 found 22 Firefox vulnerabilities, including 14 high-severity issues, and Mozilla shipped fixes to users in Firefox 148. That is exciting for security teams and unsettling for everyone else, because the same models are getting better at both finding and eventually exploiting weak points.
